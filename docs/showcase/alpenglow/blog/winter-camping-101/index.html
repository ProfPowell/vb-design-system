Winter Camping 101: Your First Cold-Weather Overnight

Gear selection for cold-weather camping — how to choose waterproof layers and shelters that handle snow loads.

By Sofia Marchetti · December 20, 2025 ·

Winter Is a Different Sport

A summer overnight forgives almost any mistake. A winter overnight forgives almost none. The same trip that would have been a mild inconvenience in July — a soaked jacket, a thin sleeping pad, a tent pitched in the wrong spot — becomes a genuine safety problem when the temperature drops below freezing and stays there for fourteen hours of darkness.

The good news is that cold-weather camping is a solved problem. Generations of mountaineers have worked out exactly what keeps a person warm, dry, and rested in the snow. You don't need to be one of them to enjoy your first winter overnight — you need the right gear, a deliberate setup routine, and a healthy respect for moisture.

This guide walks through the three systems that matter most: how you dress, where you sleep, and how you stay warm through the night.

Layering: Choosing Waterproof Layers

Everything about winter clothing comes down to managing moisture. You generate it by working — breaking trail, pitching camp — and the cold turns it against you the moment you stop. The layering system exists to move sweat away from your skin while keeping weather out.

The Three Layers

  1. Base layer. Merino wool or a synthetic next to the skin. Its only job is to move moisture off your body. Cotton is dangerous here — it holds water and steals heat.
  2. Insulating mid layer. Fleece or a synthetic puffy that traps warm air. Carry two thin layers rather than one thick one so you can fine-tune as your effort changes.
  3. Waterproof shell. A waterproof-breathable jacket and pants are the outer wall. They block wind and wet snow while letting interior vapor escape.
Why "waterproof-breathable" matters: A fully waterproof but non-breathable shell traps your own sweat, soaking your insulation from the inside. In winter, breathability is not a luxury — it's what keeps your warm layers dry.

Reading a Shell Spec Sheet

When you choose a shell, two numbers describe how it handles water. The first is the hydrostatic head — how much water pressure the fabric resists before it leaks. The second is the moisture vapor transmission rate — how fast your sweat escapes. For winter, look for waterproof layers rated at or above the figures below.

Minimum waterproof-breathable ratings by activity
Activity Waterproof rating Breathability
Resort & car camping10,000 mm10,000 g/m²
Backcountry overnight20,000 mm15,000 g/m²
Expedition / heavy snow28,000 mm20,000 g/m²

Shelters That Handle Snow Loads

Your tent is the difference between a hard night and a dangerous one. Three-season tents are built for ventilation and bug netting; they are not built to carry the weight of accumulating snow, and a heavy overnight fall can collapse one onto its occupants.

What Makes a Four-Season Shelter

  • Stronger poles. More poles, often crossing in extra arcs, spread snow loads across the canopy instead of concentrating them on a single ridgeline.
  • Steep walls. A near-vertical wall sheds snow before it can pile up. Flatter panels collect it.
  • Solid fabric panels. Less mesh means less spindrift blowing into your sleeping space.
  • Generous guy-out points. Anchoring every guy line keeps the structure rigid under wind and weight.

Site Selection and Pitching

  1. Pick a sheltered spot out of the prevailing wind — behind trees, a ridge, or a snow wall you build yourself.
  2. Stamp out a flat platform with your boots or skis and let it harden for fifteen minutes. Firm snow holds a stake far better than loose powder.
  3. Use snow stakes, or bury "deadman" anchors — a stuff sack or stick buried sideways in packed snow holds enormous force.
  4. Tension every guy line. A taut shelter sheds snow and wind; a slack one flaps, sags, and collects.
  5. Knock accumulated snow off the walls before bed and once during the night if it's still falling.

The Sleep System

You lose more heat to the ground than to the air, which is why your sleeping pad matters as much as your bag. Pads are rated by R-value, a measure of how well they resist heat loss. For snow, you want a total R-value of 5 or higher — and you can simply stack two pads to get there.

Sleeping pad R-value guidance by lowest expected temperature
Lowest temp Target R-value Typical setup
20 °F / −7 °C3–4One insulated pad
0 °F / −18 °C5–6Insulated pad + foam pad
−20 °F / −29 °C7+Two insulated pads stacked

Pair the pad with a bag rated about 10 °F colder than the coldest night you expect. The rating is a survival figure, not a comfort one, and the margin covers a tired body that isn't generating much heat. A bag liner adds warmth cheaply and keeps body oils off the insulation.

Staying Warm Through the Night

Gear gets you most of the way; habits cover the rest. A few small routines make the difference between shivering until dawn and sleeping soundly.
Eat a fat-heavy snack before bed

Digestion is internal heat. A handful of nuts, cheese, or a spoon of nut butter gives your body slow-burning fuel to run the furnace through the coldest hours. Going to bed hungry almost guarantees a cold night.

Sleep with tomorrow's layers — and your boot liners

Stuff the next day's base layer and your boot liners into the bottom of your sleeping bag. They'll be warm and dry in the morning, and you'll skip the brutal shock of cold clothing at dawn.

Vent before you sweat

If you wake up damp, you're too warm. Open a zipper or shed a layer before you start sweating. Moisture trapped in your insulation overnight is the single most common cause of a cold, miserable winter camp.

Make a hot water bottle

Fill a leakproof bottle with hot (not boiling) water, wrap it in a sock, and tuck it between your thighs or at your feet. It radiates heat for hours and doubles as unfrozen drinking water in the morning.

Before You Go

  • Tell someone your plan — route, campsite, and return time. Winter margins are thin.
  • Check the avalanche forecast for any terrain steeper than 30 degrees, and the weather window for the whole trip.
  • Carry more water capacity than you think. Melting snow for water is slow, fuel-hungry work — bring extra fuel.
  • Pack a real headlamp and spare batteries. Cold drains cells fast, and the nights are long.
  • Keep electronics and a spare battery warm in an inside pocket so they survive the night.

When the trip is over, dry everything completely before storage — the same rules from our Complete Gear Care Guide apply double in winter, when a single damp stow can ruin a down bag.

"Nobody is born a winter camper. You earn it one cold, well-prepared night at a time — and then you never want the season to end."

— Sofia Marchetti, Alpenglow Field Education Lead
